Finding the Humanity in The Epic of Gilgamesh

September 15, 2025


I've been thinking a lot lately about the roots of storytelling - more specifically, about how much of what we enjoy today is timeless.

The classics have (usually) endured for good reasons. My greater hypothesis is this: by studying the oldest stories, I can better recognize what fundamentals of storytelling resonate on a deep, human level for audiences. As a bonus, I also get a little history lesson. (Context is key, after all.)

And so, I recently found myself doing some shopping in New York City. I found myself with a copy of David Ferry's translation of the Epic of Gilgamesh at a decent price (by Manhattan standards), and a four-hour train delay. As it turned out, I was surprised how readable I found the Epic - reading a story entirely in poetic verse both keeps the information-density high and the sections light. No better point of study than the oldest surviving story, I suppose...

Time and Character

The Epic has a surprising sense of relative time in its storytelling. The first tablet opens with a description of Gilgamesh that comes across as a sort of eulogy or remembrance:

"Of him, who knew the most of all men know; who made the journey - heartbroken, reconciled. Who knew the way things were before the Flood - the secret things, the mystery. Who went to the end of the earth (and over), who returned and wrote the story on a tablet of stone."

Time plays a key role in the Epic, as it relates to its largest theme - mortality and legacy. Gilgamesh's character arc is one of a humbled demigod - mighty and self-interested at first, more somber and thoughtful at the end. Compare an earlier description in Tablet 1:

"There was no withstanding the aura or power of the Wild Ox Gilgamesh. Neither the father's son nor the wife of the noble; neither the mother's daughter nor the warrior's bride was safe."

to his monologue to Siduri the tavern-keeper in Tablet 10:

"I look like one who was undergone a journey, like one whose grief lives in his heart. I wander the wilderness wearing the skin of a beast because I grieve for the death of Enkidu the companion, he who was fought with lions and with wolves."

(To be clear, Gilgamesh never quite reaches anything resmbling a sympathetic protagonist by modern standards - he is a brute, a rapist, and generally loathed by his people. To know how much of his character is intended to be admirable in the time it was written would require more anthropological knowledge than I have.)
